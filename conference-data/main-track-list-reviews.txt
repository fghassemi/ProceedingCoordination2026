---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
The paper presents a distributed algorithm for
federated ILP learning.

The motivation is to support explainable learning in
the context of learning from multiple private data
sources.

Being based on logical inference, inductive logic
programming (ILP) addresses explainability. Existing
ILP systems (Popper is an example) are centralized.

The paper provides some background on ILP and Bach, a
coordination language based on ask/tell shared data
store for communication.

The three algorithms are presented. Algorithm 1 models
the Popper centralized algorithm for ILP. The idea is to
generate hypotheses evaluate them against sets of
positive an negative examples and a background knowledge,
update the knowledge of the generation process and repeat
until a satisfactory hypothesis is found.

Algorithms 2 and 3 specify server and client roles of a
distributable form of Algorithm 1 using the Bach ask/tell
store for communication. Roughtly, the server generates
hypotheses and posts them in the store, the clients
evaluate each hypothesis against their local data and
post the results, then the server combines the local
evaluation results revises the generation process and
repeats until done.

The proof of correctness begins by defining constraints
on the distributed data and examples to be a federated
IPL system. The proof shows that the outcome of
centralized learning is the same as that for distribute
learning if the distributed form satisfies the conditions
of being a federated ILP learning system.

Experiments based an 3 data sets from ILP learning
community benchmarks are reported, comparing outcomes
and execution time of the centralized and distributed
algorithms. As expected, what is learned is the same
while distributed learning has performance penalties as expected. One source of delay is seems to be
due  to use of the waiting mechanism of Bach.

There is a brief discussion of related work.

Strengths:
The paper addresses an important problem.
The algorithms provide an elegant specification of the
essence of centralized and distributed algorithms.
The proof of correctness is well organized and
seems to be sound (its a bit informal is some places).

Weaknesses:
Conditions that distribution of the learning algorithm parameters
must satisfy to be a federated partition seem rather restrictive.  An argument that these conditions are
met in real situations would be good.

The related work seems to compare the learning algorithm
features more than the distribution method. It mentions
on other coordination based method but doesn't address
perfomance. What about other distributed computing
methods?


Some details:

You talk about a predicate p -- what do you mean by
'predicate'?  A boolean or a function with boolean
result?

Algorithm 1 uses the variable CC which is unbound
and has both all caps and lower case forms.
Algorithm 2 has an assignment CC = C.  ?

The E_i appear out of the blue when you introduce
the distributed algorithm.  It would be good to
give some hint as to how they are picked or
at least a forward reference to the proof.

Proposition 2. final sentence -- E+? did you mean
e+?

---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
This paper describes, formalizes, and evaluates Bach4Popper, a distributed system for inductive logic programming (ILP). The system is described both through Python (pseudo?) code and as a concurrent process. The paper develops formally conditions for distributed ILP to be possible and demonstrates empirically that Bach4Popper succeeds on some examples.

The Bach4Popper system itself seems interesting, and I think its goals are laudable (even if I am somewhat dubious about the proposed motivation that ILP can universally provide a more explainable alternative to statistical approaches to machine learning). However, I found this paper somewhat strangely organized, and found it difficult to draw a coherent conclusions.

The paper presents both a theoretical argument that distributed ILP is possible, and practical validation that distributed ILP succeeds on known (small) examples. The former makes the latter seem unsurprising. From the theoretical results, it seems like the only potential difficulty would be in the construction of a federated partition, but this is not part of the evaluation. (Also, it is not clear to me whether it is difficult to generate consistent partitions of the background knowledge and the positive and negative examples.)

The paper also presents performance evaluation of the distributed ILP approach. The primary result here seems to be that coordination can introduce non-negligible overhead, but again this seems to speak mainly to the size of the examples.

As a result, I find it difficult to support including the paper as-is. I agree that the system under development is interesting, and would enjoy reading a paper that provided a more thorough and more contextualized evaluation.

The paper identifies a problem with the existing approaches to bridge blockchains working with different synchronicity assumptions. Naive implementations lead to delays that may affect liveness properties like finalisation and ultimately, consensus.

The main contribution is a task scheduler that bridges these heterogeneous blockchains, optimising query times based on an estimated probability of asynchronous consensus completion. Relevance (to the conference topics): I believe this contribution is off-topic, as the references strongly suggest (most are in systems conferences and journals). Soundness (rigour and validity): the technicalities seem solid, but the lack of details and of a more in-depth evaluation with a representative and significant benchmark hinders the validation and the empirical conclusions. ---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
The paper studies the problem of scheduling queries in cross-chain bridges involving asynchronous blockchains, with the goal of efficiently determining when a transaction has reached finality. The authors model the problem using a belief-state Semi-Markov Decision Process derived from a partially observable setting, and apply optimal stopping theory together with Bayesian estimation to derive a query scheduling policy. The approach is supported by an implementation and an empirical evaluation using representative blockchain protocols, showing improvements over a naive polling strategy.

The paper addresses a relevant problem, and provides a practical implementation and empirical results supporting the approach. However, the topic can only marginally fit in the COORDINATION scope, being just loosely connected to the topic "Decentralised systems, Peer-to-peer computing, Smart Contracts & Blockchain Applications". Furthermore, the paper is very hard to follow for readers who are not experts in blockchain systems, as expected for most of the COORDINATION community, as it lacks a presentation of the approach from the ground up. Furthermore, the novelty with respect to existing literature is unclear, especially given the large body of related work discussed but not thoroughly differentiated. Finally, the evaluation is significantly hindered by only comparing with a very naive baseline. Overall, based on the limitations of the current presentation, it is difficult for me to be convinced that the contribution is sufficiently novel and relevant for the COORDINATION community.

## Main suggestions for improvement

- Improve clarity and accessibility, especially for readers not deeply familiar with blockchain systems, by framing the problem and system model assumed from the ground up. For instance, what is a two-way peg-based sidechain?
- Better position the work with respect to related literature, for instance by adding a comparison table summarizing the features of the existing approaches, and clearly highlighting the added value of this work with respect to them.
- Strengthen the experimental evaluation by including more advanced baselines beyond naive fixed-interval polling (e.g., what about a randomised exponential-time polling that is more common in general network requests?), and possibly more distributed agents.
- Discuss further how this is to be considered a coordination problem on a distributed blockchain.

## More comments

- Fig2, what is max/min variance and all values? It needs more explanation.
- At some point in the paper, it is said that \Delta t comes from a finite set of possible times T. However, later it seems to just be a single fixed time. What is true?
- Why s starts from s_0, but then resets to 0 (and not s_0) after a failed polling?
- Why waiting always for a fixed duration \Delta t updating s without polling, instead of just computing how much time you should wait to trigger the next polling, and then poll?

## Summary

This paper introduces HistMSO, a monadic second-order logic (MSO) for reasoning about histories and abstract executions, the execution models for replicated data systems introduced by Burckhardt. The authors demonstrate that HistMSO can express 39 out of 42 consistency models from the Viotti and Vukolic hierarchy, and they develop a translation of HistMSO satisfiability and model-checking to MSO over words, enabling the use of the MONA tool for automated reasoning. The translation is extended from plain histories to abstract executions under two restricting assumptions: real-time arbitration and k-transient visibility. Decidability results follow as corollaries for finite and omega-infinite histories and for real-time, k-transient abstract executions. The paper also establishes a bound on the cutwidth of the interval graph of a history in terms of the number of processes, drawing a connection to prior work on tree decompositions of message-passing communication models.

## Strengths

**1. Clear and significant contribution.** The paper delivers several concrete results: the HistMSO logic itself, expressibility of 39 out of 42 consistency models, a sound and complete translation to MONA, decidability corollaries, and a cutwidth bound. Each contribution is stated precisely and proved or sketched with sufficient rigour for a conference paper.

**2. Strong fit with COORDINATION scope.** The paper sits at the intersection of coordination models, concurrency theory, and automated verification, all of which are core topics of COORDINATION. The link between consistency models for replicated data and coordination languages is well-motivated and natural.

**3. Broad expressibility.** Encoding 39 out of 42 consistency models from a well-established hierarchy is a compelling coverage result. The paper is transparent about the three exceptions (models relying on exact timing constraints) and even sketches a path toward handling them via discrete time semantics.

**4. Leverage of an established tool.** Grounding the approach in MONA is a deliberate and justified choice. The connection to the automata-theoretic tradition and to prior results on tree decompositions of message-sequence charts gives the work a solid theoretical pedigree and opens concrete directions for tool-supported verification.

**5. Well-structured proofs.** The cutwidth section (Section 5) is technically careful. The construction of the generator graph via an explicit algorithm, the bounding of in- and out-degrees, and the proof of Theorem 3 are all clearly presented. The omitted proofs are collected in Appendix B with full detail.

## Weaknesses

**1. Discrepancy in the expressibility count.** The abstract states that 39 out of 42 consistency models are expressible, while the body of the paper (Section 3.2, contribution 2 in the introduction) states 36. This inconsistency is not explained anywhere in the paper and undermines confidence in the claimed result. The authors must resolve this before publication.

**2. k-transient visibility is a strong assumption.** The extension to abstract executions relies on k-transient visibility, a restriction that rules out executions where the visibility of an operation to a remote process stabilises only after more than k steps. The paper argues that the assumption is needed to avoid undecidability, and the analogy with FIFO / PRAM is helpful, but there is no discussion of which of the 39 expressible consistency models are actually verifiable under this assumption. It is conceivable that several of the more interesting models require unbounded transient behaviour in practice.

**3. No experimental evaluation.** The paper positions MONA as a practical theorem prover for consistency models, but no experiments are reported. There is no benchmarking, no running time data, and no worked example showing MONA actually discharging a consistency property end-to-end. The conclusion acknowledges this gap and lists tool implementation as immediate future work, but for a paper that makes usability a selling point, the absence is notable.

**4. The encoding is only sketched.** Section 4.2 describes the timeline encoding and the attribute encoding in reasonably clear terms, but the translation of formulas is only sketched ("we omit the full definition of the translation for brevity"), and the formula isEncoding is not given. Theorem 1 is stated without proof. For a paper whose central technical claim rests on the correctness of this translation, this is a significant gap. A proof sketch or a pointer to a technical report with full details would be necessary.

**5. Presentation of the consistency model encodings is thin.** Section 3.2 presents only a handful of consistency models (RVal, RealTime, Linearizability, Quiescent Consistency) and defers the rest to the appendix and to the Viotti and Vukolic source. Given that the expressibility claim is a main contribution, a slightly more systematic treatment, even in a table, would significantly strengthen the paper.

## Questions for the Authors

1. The abstract claims 39 expressible models while the body claims 36. Which is the correct number, and which three models (beyond those based on exact timing) account for the discrepancy?

2. Among the 39 (or 36) expressible models, how many remain decidable under the real-time and k-transient visibility restrictions? Is there a subset that is verifiable end-to-end with MONA without further assumptions?

3. The bound on cutwidth is 2m^2 where m is the number of processes. How does this translate in practice? For typical distributed systems benchmarks with, say, 3 to 10 processes, is the resulting tree decompositions width manageable for MONA?

4. The conclusion mentions offline and centralised monitoring as a limitation. Have the authors considered whether the cutwidth bound could support a decomposed, process-local monitoring strategy, analogous to the bounded asynchrony results cited from prior work?

## Minor Comments

- Section 3.2: the formula for Finite(O) appears to contian a typo; the disjunction $\neg \exists a \in O \;\vee\; \exists a \in O.\; \forall b \in O.\; b.\mathit{stime} \leq a.\mathit{stime}$ reads oddly. The first disjunct seems to handle the empty set, but the logical structure should be clarified.
- Section 4.2, Remark 2: the simplifying assumption that no two operation extremities share a timestamp is reasonable, but a brief argument that this assumption does not change the MSO theory would be welcome.
- The related work discussion is placed inside the conclusion section. For a paper of this technical depth, a dedicated related work section would improve readability.
- A few notation inconsistencies appear between the main text and the appendix (for example, the session order arrow sorr in the appendix is not introduced in the main text).
- The use of reference [21] (Nipkow and Weber, 2020) alongside [19] (Klarlund and Moller, 2001) for MONA is slightly confusing; it would help to clarify it in the text which citation refers to the tool implementation and which to the user manual.

## Recommendation

This is a solid theoretical paper making genuine contributions to automated reasoning about consistency models for replicated data systems. The logic is well-designed, the expressibility result is broad, the decidability results are clearly derived, and the cutwidth analysis is technically sound. The main concerns are the unexplained discrepancy in the expressibility count, the incompleteness of the encoding description, and the absence of any experimental validation.

I suggest accepting the paper, but the authors should resolve the count discrepancy.

Providing at least a proof sketch or full reference for the encoding translation will make the paper much stronger.

This paper introduces a framework for formal verification of distributed, data-intensive microservice applications using Petri Nets.  In particular, the authors propose a framework that automatically 1) mines object-centric Petri nets (OCPN) from event logs, and 2) evaluates formal properties using temporal logic specification in CTL. The paper runs the framework for the analysis of a case study and assess the validity of the approach.

Relevance: The paper is relevant for the conference, it falls under the scope of the conference and matches the main topics of interest.

Significance: The contribution to the scientific community is significant as the paper addresses the problem of formally verifying properties in complex heterogeneous distributed systems, with a high impact in commercial and industrial pipelines.

Originality: The originality and novelty of the contribution consist of the verification framework, which combines mining OCPNs and verification via CTL. The novelty is limited. Neither the models (OCPNs) nor the mining algorithms are novel. Petri nets have been used for modelling and analysis distributed systems for a long time. In the context of microservice orchestration, previous papers already have addressed the use of Petri net and simulation/runtime checking, but neither OCPNs nor CTL.

Soundness: My major concern with this paper is the soundness, validity and generalization of the experiments in the case study (the authors already pointed it out these concerns in the "thread to validity" section). The logs are generated from a simulation of real-world SAP transactions.

Petri net models that are mined from event logs are "too simple" as they are constrained to 1-safe nets. The use of 1-safe nets, together with CTL, prevents the specification of fairness constraints. Some metrics show poor performance (e.g., precision of some Petri net elements such as "Products" in "Order Management" event logs).

Reproducibility: The scripts, dataset and tutorials for running the experiments are publicly available (although I have not exhaustively tested he reproducibility of the results).

Overall quality: In general, the text is readable and the quality of the presentation is good. Some extra citations are welcome (e.g. references to Coloured Petri Net publications).

Summary

This tool paper describes the NeoGoddar tool for static deadlock detection and repair in Go programs.
Go source code is abstracted through existing tools into a CCS representation, which is then used to detect deadlocks and propose fixes.
Neither soundness nor correctness are formally investigated, but the tool seems to produce useful results on a collection of practical examples.

Comments
=========
The part about “fixing” (resolving) deadlocks through code-changes is interesting and the authors of course acknowledge that it changes program semantics. I would really like to read more about this, and in how far the `fixer` can take other (even syntactic) dependencies (e.g. of LHS/read values) into account. Of course, without any specification of what the original program was intended to do, we can only guess if the new behaviour is “correct” or not…

the article acknowledges an MSc thesis ([2], supervised by one of the authors) on “Goddar”, an alternative attempt at the same problem. Now, as an academic who has published work with MSc-students before, I understand that this is most likely not a deliberate plagiarism attempt. It is clear that we in our profession make often incremental developments on top of earlier work by means of extensive reuse of the code-base. Here, I am missing a clear separation from and comparison to Goddar: while the article compares to alternative tools (Tables 1 & 2), a comparison to Goddard is conspicuously missing. In fact, I have been unable to determine differences between Goddar and NeoGoddar – possibly handling channels with capacities?

The format as a tool paper plus the issue of the relation to [2] make it difficult to judge originality.

This paper presentas a tool to find (and propose fixes to some) deadlocks in Go. Go, through goroutines, offers powerful concurent programming primitives. The section ends with the evaluation of the deadlock resolution system. In this case the only competition (GFix) solves a problem that they do not try to solve, and they solve a problem that GFix does not address. The solved problems are addressed by adding "more concurrency" to avoid deadlocks which is very interesting. While not attempting the case of missing communications, as it hard to argue the correctness of sending bogus values to keep the programs running.

This paper describes the design and implementation of the tool. It contains a good evaluation. However, the soundness, dependency on unmaintained tools, and fragility of the analysis when dealing with infinite loops are all issues that are left for future work. I have to say that I look forwards the future work, but this paper has a good amount of interesting material and discussion.

- The goal of the paper is to improve an IoT system with adaptive features during the design phase. Specifically, the focus is on indoor air quality, which is certainly an important topic.

- The authors use their own tools: the YODA multi-agent language and Sibilla forecasting tools. Why are these tools particularly well-suited for this task, and not others? For example, the Actor Model? The paper appears to be the engineering of a use case using proprietary tools.

- Ad page.2: The sentence "If the level goes under a certain threshold, or even if there are people in a room, the timer is ignored to analyze the air quality" seems to suggest that the system is not purely time-driven but also adaptive. However, the logic of this statement is unclear, since IAQ cannot serve as a trigger for a measurement, as it is itself the result of the measurement process.

- What would the integration of other modeling tools (besides YODA) look like in Sibilla?

- How large can a scenario become? The one shown is extremely small. For example, with regard to the number of devices, rooms, and more complex adaptation requirements?

- The "Related Work" section highlights some systems, but there is no systematic approach to what was being sought. Furthermore, "Related Work" should refer to simulations of the given use case, not the tools themselves, because the paper does not present new tools or methods. The authors' conclusion of the section is that "scalability is challenging" -- but the scenario presented does not demonstrate why the proposed solution is "scalable." It is also unclear what exactly is meant by "scalable", perhaps the scalability of the designs? The authors themselves omit many parameters in their scenario "to avoid complexity."

- Ad section 3.1: Please explain the "Nepero constant" and its purpose here, as well as VOC, NOx, CH₂O, PM2.5, PM10 (although these are not considered). What other parameters are important that have been omitted to avoid complexity, and how relevant would these be for a realistic scenario?

- How is it demonstrated that the model and the abstraction (i.e., the selected parameters) are correct?

- The model would need to be compared with a real-world implementation to determine how good it is.

- Is people’s movement completely random? Wouldn’t it make more sense to consider realistic movement patterns?

- What happens if the "battery is drained"? Is there a warning signal before that happens?

- Most of the paper is devoted to describing the implementation of the use case scenario in great detail (including many parameter names that are of no interest to anyone, some of which are not used later in the model (e.g., "batteryCapacity") or are self-explanatory anyway, and parameter values for which no explanation is provided as to why those specific values were chosen). Especially, Listing 1.7, "The command that we can use in a Python environment to perform the simulation," and Listing 1.8, "Commands for Tracing the IAQ Scenario in a Python environment," are unnecessary and, if included at all, should be placed in an appendix.

- Regarding the simulation: Why were C1, C2, and C3 specifically chosen? Why are these three significant?

- The results of the simulation are not incorrect but highly trivial and obvious. For example, the more people in the room, the higher the CO2 consumption; the more frequently measurements must be taken, the higher the battery consumption, etc.

- No interpretations or lessons learned are drawn from the simulation results. It is also unclear what specific improvements regarding the IoT environment can be derived from the results.

- It would be better if the authors focused more on the use of the mentioned and possible formal analysis capabilities of Sibilla.

- In summary, this paper describes the simulation of a very specific use case scenario in detail. Its research contribution is unclear. No new methods or tools are presented. Nor is there a comparison explaining why the authors’ tools are better than others. The contribution is the simulation of a very specific use case. Is this use case easily extendable and generalizable to other scenarios? And if so, which ones?

# Summary

The authors describe a use-case implemented in YODA and Sibilla, simulating the Indoor-Air-Quality of set of interconnected rooms. The underlying model uses number of "devices" with batteries and sensors, and a number of "humans" that enter and leave rooms. The rooms are structured in a fixed graph topology. The full implementation is available online.

The goal of this paper, from my understanding, is to illustrate the usefulness of YODA to model and analyse such systems with many symmetric collaborating components, known as collective adaptive systems.


# General comments

I found the ideas to be interesting and well suited to COORDINATION, and the manuscript to be well structured and to include a good/rich overview of related work.

However, the amount of scientific contribution is relatively low, given that (in my opinion) there were no new concepts or tool improvements introduced in this work. However, given that COORDINATION welcomes tool papers, I would position this closer to a tool/experience-report manuscript. A better motivation could have highlighted better the contributions.

Hence I propose (weak) acceptance of this work.

More detailed comments to the authors follow below.


# Detailed comments to the authors

## Goal of this paper
You mention that the goal is "to propose an approach that can be used during the design phase of an IoT ecosystem". I personally find that this paper acts as a tutorial or experience report on how one can use YODA + Sibilla. I.e., I believe that there some aspects were not clear:
 - how to **generalise** your implementation into a generic "approach" (which is promised in the introduction);
 - how **needed** was the use-case, e.g., if there was any partner (such as an IoT company or an institution) that was expecting to benefit from the data, or if there as an active research project that involved this air-quality question;
 - how **useful** was the use-case, i.e., if there was some validation of the final results, confirming that the chosen parameters were sensible one.

As a research paper, I expected more scientific contributions or conclusions, or more novelties on the approach (e.g., new tools, features, or concepts).

However, given that COORDINATION welcomes tool papers, I would position this paper more towards a tool/experience paper, and less as a research paper, which I find acceptable at this venue.

## Timing of events and communication details
Given the distributed nature of the example, it was not clear to me the precise notion of when actions were taken place. You mention that "every scheduled time" a device reads the environment, updates its state, and reacts. However, it is not clear if
  (1) the full simulation evolves in synchronised rounds,
  (2) agents evolve with different (fixed or changing) scheduling times,
  (3) agents can communicate with each other explicitly without using the environment, or
  (4) race-conditions can happen due to different interpretations or different timings to observe.

## Structure of the technical aspects
I found the bottom-up presentation, starting with details and escalating to the big picture, **not** optimal for the reader (in my personal opinion). I would have appreciated to have a clearer picture of the agents and their communication skills using, e.g., some form of component or deployment diagram. The paper describes many details that require the reader to reverse engineer and mentally build the big picture slowly.

In Section 3 you start with 1.5 pages of introduction, followed by half page for Subsection 3.1, and in this introduction you do not help navigating the rest of the section. I would personally suggest to have a smaller introduction, explaining the structure of that section, and have a subsection dedicated to the overall picture (clarifying who are the agents (_i_ devices and _j_ humans) and what is an environment in YODA).

A large of your effort is to motivate why your abstraction is well-suited for modelling indoor-air-quality, suggesting this is a core contribution of the paper, but it is not clear if there is any validation (external partner, real measurements, or something else).

## Features and reactions
I had problems understanding what is a "feature", and how the devices send information (or update the environment). Although a feature is declared in an agent, it does not seem to be read or modified by the agent, and the environment does not seem to read/write any local variable of the agents.

Some better explanations of the interactions mechanisms, and the roles of features, would be welcomed.

## Graph structure
Your implementation uses a graph to describe room locations. This graph is implicit in the behaviour of the "Human"s, in a very primitive approach (in my opinion). Having a strtucture for an array of edges, or something more structured, could help motivating the use of YODA to model IoT systems with notions of topology/geographical-location. Currently it gives the feeling it is not optimal for that (specially for dynamic topologies), although it is likely that it can indeed handle such notions better than it seems from this paper.

## Simulating the system
YODA provides an compact notation (DSL) to describe agents and the environment. However, there is no DSL to instantiate and configure these agents, which has to be done within a Python environment.

Why not having a dedicated DSL to describe the full simulation? This could improve the usability of the tool (in my view), avoiding the dependency on knowing Python to run YODA.

## Measures of the simulations
Your evaluations help understanding how to:
 - analyse the final state of many random runs of a scenario, and
 - analyse a concrete random run of a scenario.

It is not clear how to analyse the intermediate states of many random runs, e.g., how often the air quality drops below a threshold (and not only the final state). You do mention that Sibilla integrates some temporal logics, but it would be good to have an idea of what could be measured in your system (even only informally with concrete examples). This would help understanding better what YODA+Sibilla could be useful for.

## Small remarks for the authors
- Section 3.1: you say you measure capacity of batteries in mA/m (miliamps per minute), but amps is already a measure of current per time unit. You probably mean mA*m (miliamp-minute) instead (althouth mAh is often more used).
- Section 3.1: you could motivate why some names are given. E.g., minH is the sensitivity of a device, but it is not clear how to read the "H" in "minH". Similar for some other names.
- the probabilities in the actions appear in Listing 1.1, but are only explained a few pages later.
- Plots in Figs.2-5: these are very difficult to read, mainly due to the small fonts; in my case I printed the paper in B&W, making it even harder to read. I recommend using plots readable in B&W and with larger fonts.
- Section 4.2: all references to figures (3-5) miss the keyword "Fig"; for example "Main Hall (3)" should be "Main Hall (Fig. 3)".

## Summary and Contributions

The paper addresses the problem of distributed multi-hop localisation in indoor environments. Such settings must contend with signal noise and loss, requiring devices to estimate their relative positions through local interactions with neighbouring devices, often in the presence of dynamic network topologies and environmental changes. The authors recast two established geometric localisation algorithms—DV-based positioning (DV-hop) and NB-cooperative localisation (NB-coop)—within the aggregate computing paradigm, to obtain modular, composable, and fully distributed self-stabilising device networks. They propose variants of these localisation algorithms and evaluate them through a series of simulated experiments, considering different communication radii, device mobility levels, and communication noise.

The paper first introduces the problem and then provides
(i) a brief overview of aggregate programming, focusing on the exchange calculus (XC) and explaining how the field calculus constructs 'share', 'nbr', and 'rep' can be encoded using the 'exchange' primitive of XC, and
(ii) a summary of multi-hop indoor localisation algorithms, focusing on DV-hop and NB-coop, two classical approaches.
The authors then present two aggregate implementations of indoor localisation. The NB-coop algorithm employs two variants, namely gradient descent and multilateration. DV-hop uses two anchor-selection strategies, dubbed 'R-Close Anchors' and 'K-Closest Anchors', which provide the distance, position, and correction information to the DV-hop algorithm.

Finally, the paper reports a simulated evaluation using the FCPP library, focusing on three main parameters:
- the communication radius, defining the distance beyond which devices cannot interact,
- the mobility speed, controlling the rate of device movement, and
- the variance parameter, modelling the relative standard error of communication noise.
The experimental results suggest that restricting DV-hop localisation to the closest anchor devices improves performance in DV-based approaches. Meanwhile, multilateration-based cooperative approaches provide the best overall trade-off between localisation accuracy, convergence speed, and robustness, while also keeping message sizes and computational costs relatively low.

## Overall Evaluation

Indoor localisation without reliance on GPS remains a significant challenge, particularly in systems composed of networks of autonomous devices that communicate asynchronously and may join or leave the network dynamically. Existing approaches to indoor localisation often assume *static network topologies* operating under stable and error-free conditions—assumptions that rarely hold in realistic settings. The authors pursue a well-motivated line of work that addresses this limitation by revisiting two classical localisation algorithms and reformulating them within the aggregate programming paradigm to improve localisation accuracy and communication efficiency in dynamic and noisy environments.

Overall, the paper tackles an interesting and relevant problem and presents a promising approach. The authors also provide a GitHub repository containing the implementation and the experiments discussed in Section 5, which is a welcome addition for reproducibility. However, the paper suffers from several omissions and presentation issues. One major concern is that it often reads more like a technical report than a distilled presentation of ideas intended to guide the reader through the main contributions. As a result, several parts are difficult to follow.

## Summary

This paper introduces DDS (Distribution Design Signature), a four-step analytical protocol for characterizing the coordination constraints of multi-agent system (MAS) models prior to their distributed implementation. Inspired by the ODD (Overview, Design concepts, Details) framework used for MAS documentation, DDS defines seven qualitative dimensions (agent-agent coupling, agent-environment coupling, temporal synchronization, shared state consistency, structural dynamics, computational load distribution, and behavioral robustness to asynchrony) across which a model is assessed. The resulting "signature" is then matched to one of five archetypal coordination patterns (Spatial Local, Global Synchronous, Event-driven Loose, Shared State Intensive, Highly Dynamic), and this matching guides reasoning about feasible distribution strategies and the exclusion of structuraly incompatible ones. The protocol is illustrated on two MAS case studies: a Rock-Paper-Scissors (RPS) spatial simulation and the Cammisol soil biogeochemistry model.

## Strengths

**1. Timely and well-motivated contribution.** The paper addresses a genuine methodological gap. Most existing work on distributed MAS focuses on implementation-level concerns (frameworks, partitioning algorithms, load balancing), leaving modelers without principled guidance at the design stage. DDS fills this gap in a practical and accessible way.

**2. Good fit with COORDINATION scope.** The paper is squarely within the conference's interests: it concerns coordination models and languages (specifically the analysis of coordination constraints), actor/agent-based systems, and high-performance distributed computing. The "coordination pattern" vocabulary it introduces aligns naturally with the conference's conceptual tradition.

**3. Empirical grounding.** The RPS case study is particularly compelling because it describes a real failure mode: using loose synchronization (GhostMod in FPMAS) led to incorrect emergent behavior, and the paper shows retrospectively how DDS would have flagged this risk at design time. This is honest and instructive.

**4. Principled structure.** The four-step protocol (characterize, match patterns, identify compatible strategies, exclude incompatible ones) is well-organized. The inclusion of diagnostic questions for each dimension is a thoughtful usability feature that lowers the barrier for non-specialists in distributed systems.

**5. Detailed appendix.** The full ODD and DDS analyses of both models provide transparency and reproducibility, which is commendable given the qualitative nature of the approach.

## Weaknesses

**1. Absence of formal foundations.** The protocol is entirely qualitative and relies on the "judgment of the modeler." No formal semantics, ordering relations between levels, or compositionality rules are defined. This is not necessarily fatal for a practical tool paper, but the paper positions DDS partly as an "analytical framework," a claim that sits uncomfortably with the absence of any mathematical underpinning. The conference audience will expect at least some discussion of how DDS could be given more rigorous foundations, even if that is deferred to future work.

**2. Steps 3 and 4 are underspecified.** The paper states that "details of the procedure are omitted here due to space limitations" for both the strategy compatibility mapping (Step 3) and the incompatibility reasoning (Step 4). These are arguably the most consequential steps of the protocol, and their omission significantly weakens the contribution. The reader cannot evaluate whether these steps are sound, let alone reproducible.

**3. Limited evaluation.** Two case studies are presented, both by the same research group and both applied retrospectively. There is no evidence that DDS produces consistent results across different analysts (inter-rater reliability), no comparison against alternative methods, and no assesment of whether following DDS recommendations leads to measurably better outcomes than ad hoc approaches. The RPS result is compelling precisely because a real failure was encountered, yet one example does not validate a general protocol.

**4. Pattern coverage and completeness.** The five coordination patterns are motivated by a literature survey, but the claim that they "cover the most frequently encountered structural configurations" is not substantiated. The paper acknowledges hybrid profiles but provides limited guidance for models that fit no dominant pattern clearly. A more systematic argument for coverage (or an honest discussion of gaps) would strengthen the contribution.

**5. Ambiguity in dimension boundaries.** Several dimension tables (e.g., Table 1) include numerical thresholds (e.g., "< 10 agents" for local coupling) presented as "orders of magnitude" and "not universal normative thresholds." This creates ambiguity: modelers may find it difficult to assign levels consistently, particulary for models near the boundaries. The diagnostic questions partially address this, but the tension between the quantitative-looking tables and the stated qualitative intent is not fully resolved.

**6. Paper category mismatch.** The submission reads as a tool/protocol paper, but the formal analysis portions of Steps 3 and 4 are absent, and no software artifact is provided. If submitted as a regular paper, reviewers will expect either more theoretical development or more thorough empirical evaluation. The authors should clarify the intended category.

## Questions for the Authors

1. Steps 3 and 4 are described but not elaborated. Could a sketch of the compatibility mapping logic be provided, even informally? Without this, it is hard to assess whether the protocol is complete.

2. Have the authors applied DDS to any model by an independent team (i.e., not the authors themselves)? If so, what was the experience in terms of consistency and usability?

3. How does DDS relate to existing model-checking or reachability-based approaches for detecting parallelization hazards? Could DDS be positioned as a lightweight pre-screening step preceding such formal analyses?

4. Is there a plan to provide DDS as a structured checklist, questionnaire tool, or software artifact to facilitate adoption?

## Minor Comments

- Section 2.4 contains a typographical error: "onceptual approaches" should be "Conceptual approaches."
- The claim in Section 4.3 that seven dimensions are "considered sufficient" is asserted without justification. Even a brief argument from the literature would strengthen this.
- Tables 9 and 11 are notably sparse compared to others. If the defining feature of a pattern is a single dominant dimension, this should be explained more explicitly rather than left implicit in the text.
- The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of DDS limitations: when might it mislead? Are there model classes where the seven dimensions are systematically insufficient?

## Recommendation

The paper presents a useful and practically motivated contribution that is well-suited to the COORDINATION audience. The core idea of systematically externalizing coordination constraints from model structure before implementation is sound and the case for it is convincing. However, the incompleteness of Steps 3 and 4, the limited empirical validation, and the absence of formal grounding leave the protocol feeling preliminary.

---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
This paper describes a reversible, asynchronous, channel-based communication protocol, inspired to some degree by the programming language Go. There are two motivating observations. The first is that errors in distributed systems are likely to arise from communicated values, and so it is important for a debugging system to be able to track the provenance of messages. The second is that many transitions in a distributed system are likely to be irrelevant to a particular error---for example, they might be exchanges of messages between unrelated proceses. So, it is important for a debugger to be able to reorder and replay transitions, only focusing on those transitions relevant to a particular failure.

To accomplish this, the paper introduces an extended syntax of processes and communications, in which:

  (i) communications are all annotated with keys, identifying their sources. Thus, for example, even in a queue of identical messages it is possible to distinguish the transitions that lead to each message.

  (ii) the states of processes, queues, and memory cells include histories, allowing individual transitions to be reversed.

The paper then defines causal and independence relationships among transitions in a system of processes, and establishes formally that rollback and replay are consistent with the system's forward semantics, and that transitions can be reordered so as to allow particular transitions to be rolled back while minimizing the total number of rollbacks required.

The paper attacks an interesting problem, and the core insights are well-motivated. I believe the technical results are correct. Therefore, I support including the paper in COORDINATION.

However, I did not find it particularly easy to read the paper or to extract value from it. Much of the paper is structured as a sequences of definitions and lemmata with little motivation or explanation. This seems particularly unfortunate given that (i) much of the machinery of the paper seems to be mostly independent of the contributions of the paper and (ii) the paper relies heavily on the reader's familiarity with other work on reversible debugging.

I have several more detailed questions about the formalization.

- It was not clear to me why backwards internal semantics and forward internal semantics needed to be presented separately. Why is the reachability hypothesis required in Lemma 1?

- History seems to be duplicated between processes, memory, and queues. For example, queues store their histories of messages. But also, processes store their individual sends and receives. Keys ensure the sends and receives of identical values are not confused. So why is the queue history itself needed?

- The must-happen-before relationship seems like it ought to be transitively closed. For example, definition 7 requires that α₁ !▷ α₂. But it seems like this would hold if α₁ = (p, i, r) and α₂ = (p, i + 2, r'). Is this intended? If so, perhaps the text could make clearer why this is okay? I assume that I am misreading, but in that case perhaps the text could be clarified about where the must-happen-before relation is closed?

- Fig 9 sets up an unannotated translation relation on systems. Definition 5 and its sequel restore the annotations. Why this two-step?

SUMMARY

The paper presents the development of verification support for the smart/legal contract language Stipula. This support is provided through a recursive function that converts a (well-formed) Stipula contract into a Java class with JML annotations. The function maps functions and events to methods, assets and fields (assumed to be of type int - they are all (implicitly) of literal types in Stipula) to fields, and initialises fields to default values. For verification purposes, parameters of methods are assigned with concrete values - the verification considers only a single scenario (symbolic analysis is not supported). The main translation function calls an auxiliary function to convert expressions (function bodies), the latter defined inductively in the grammar rules of Stipula syntax.

The main challenge is the translation of timed events and the associated implicit behavioural automaton (capturing a contract as a state machine where functions and events change state, the key idea underlying Stipula's transition semantics). To soundly support this automaton in the generated Java code, the authors introduce a mechanism to manage timed events, a "dispatch table" where entries are ordered by event creation, with the moment they should be triggered. A method in the generated Java class looks up in this table for the next event to be executed, that with the minimum time value. The (possibly non-deterministic) selection of a function to execute and the ticking of a clock to pass time and select events is modelled with JML annotations.

The key soundness result is a form of operational correspondence: every finite sequence of function or event invocation leading to a final state in the Stipula automaton (thus, an acyclic one) corresponds to a sequence of method calls in the Java code, matching one-to-one Stipula actions with Java actions. Completeness is stated as a conjecture. The soundness result extends to cyclic contracts, provided that invariants are added "by hand" to the JML contract produced by the translation function, considering only symbolic parameters not changing for the recursive function calls (due to limitations in KeY), and a bound on the number of iterations.


ASSESSMENT

Relevance (to the conference topics).
The contribution fits well the scope of COORDINATION - smart contract languages are coordination languages - and provides means to formally verify contracts, fitting thus in topic A (theoretical models and foundations of coordination).

Significance (scientific impact).
High. Equipping the Stipula framework with verification facilities that are based on a mature deductive verification platform as KeY is an important contribution. Moreover, the approach may extend to other contract languages. The translation approach has the advantage of conveying mostly automatic verification, apart from the usual need to provide invariants when dealing with recursion/iteration.
A drawback is the lack of evaluation of the approach with a significant set of contracts, possibly inspired by existing benchmarks like Rosetta.

Originality (novelty and non-incrementality).
The section on related work refers to (some) other instances of embedding languages in verification platforms, an approach popular and used for several mainstream programming languages. The original aspect here is doing so for a contract language with strong control policies and with both functions and timed events. The technical approach used seems novel. The work was not presented before and is thus not incremental (the mechanism is defined entirely in this paper).

Soundness (rigour and validity).
The paper is technically competent, and all formalities seem well defined, though I miss several "sanity checks":
1. The translation assumes that contracts are well-formed, since the function definition does not obviously show how it fails otherwise.
2. A basic soundness result would be to show that the translation always generates syntactically correct Java programs.
3. Stipula transition semantics is presented without any kind of supporting result, ensuring it makes sense and works properly.
4. Stipula contracts implicitly define a form of automata, but these are not defined. They might be inferred from the semantics and/or from the code, but they only appear as graphical drawings supporting the presentation. Are they related to some existing form of behavioural type or multi-party automaton?
5. Definition 1 describes cyclic contracts via sequences of executions passing by the same state, but since the transition system is infinite state, determining if a contract is cyclic is a decidable property?

Still on soundness, two extra notes:
- why not express the soundness results as a (co-inductive) trace simulation relation?
- getting a completeness of the operational correspondence seems feasible. The authors mention that it requires a thorough formalisation of Java semantics, which probably already exists for the fragment considered in the translation.

Reproducibility.
The paper does not clarify if an artefact was submitted, but a tool should exist and could be made public. Should the paper be accepted, it would be important to incorporate the tool in the Stipula framework (as the authors probably plan to).

Overall quality (including presentation).
The paper is easy to read, clear, and correct (as far as I could check). The contribution is an advance beyond the state-of-the-art, and the dispatch table idea could be used by other transpilations of timed event-driven languages in languages without them. To provide an extra layer of solidity, some key results (as the ones mentioned above) should be added. Moreover, I find the list of contributions promising more than the paper delivered:
- How is the adequacy of the dispatch tables 'for representing time
constraints and event scheduling within a deductive verification setting' judged?
- Shouldn't the suitability of the translation 'for a wide range of contractual behaviours' be evaluated rigorously with a significant benchmark?
- how does the soundness of the translation ensure that the properties verified in KeY reflect the semantics of the original contract? Doesn't such a result (that I couldn't find stated formally) require a proof of property satisfaction reflection?

Minor Comments.
- The abstract (namely, the first sentence) could benefit from being rewritten to be more informative.
- The list of contributions at the end of the introduction could point to the results presented.
- The presentation of Stipula and of Key could benefit from being intertwined with examples, illustrating the features presented.
- The paragraph just before Theorem 2 mentions 'the acyclic case', but it should say 'cyclic', right?

## Summary and Contributions

The paper introduces Phyelds, an implementation of an aggregate computing framework for Python. Phyelds aims to bridge the tooling gap between aggregate computing on the one hand and the Python ecosystem for data science and machine learning on the other. The framework provides a lightweight yet fully featured implementation of the field calculus computation model and integrates it with Python's rich data-science and machine-learning ecosystem through its API.

The authors provide an overview of the aggregate programming paradigm and describe the architecture of Phyelds, explaining how the main constructs of the field calculus are realised within the framework. The paper concludes with several case studies that demonstrate how the Phyelds library can express common aggregate computing patterns, integrate machine-learning models, and interact with third-party simulators.

## Overall Evaluation

A current research direction explores the integration of aggregate programming with machine learning. Python has effectively become the de facto programming language for data science and machine learning due to its rich ecosystem of libraries and tools. Phyelds leverages this by providing a framework that *natively* bridges aggregate computing with the Python ecosystem. This gives Phyelds a practical advantage over existing aggregate programming frameworks (e.g., Protelis), which do not target Python and, therefore, require additional interoperability layers to integrate with Python-based machine-learning workflows.

The introduction is well motivated, and the overview of aggregate computing is concise, informative, and largely self-contained. The authors do a good job explaining the overall architecture of Phyelds and how its components interact. The paper adopts a systematic approach by describing how each operator of the field calculus is realised in the framework, which helps clarify the design decisions behind the implementation. Finally, the inclusion of several case studies effectively illustrates how Phyelds can be used in practice.

Overall, the work addresses an interesting and relevant problem and presents a promising tool. The paper would benefit from clearer positioning with respect to existing aggregate programming frameworks. This could naturally be addressed through a short related work section discussing systems such as Protelis and other comparable tools, highlighting the practical differences and advantages of Phyelds. In addition, the paper would be strengthened by discussing the main design challenges encountered during the implementation of the framework and how these were addressed. Section 3, which describes the implementation of Phyelds and the realisation of the field calculus operators, would be a natural place to include such a discussion.

## Strengths

+ Well-motivated problem.

+ Clearly identified tooling gap.

+ Convincing set of case studies.

+ Largely self-contained presentation.

## Issues and Weaknesses

- The paper currently focuses on the engineering details of the framework. The contribution would be stronger if the authors discussed the main design challenges encountered when implementing such a framework and how these challenges were addressed (would fit nicely in Section 3).

- The paper lacks a 'Related Work' section (place it in the Conclusion section).

## Presentation and Suggestions for Improvement

- Section 3 (Calculus Module): Please expand or clarify the following points:
  - It is unclear how the 'transform_code' function operates.
  - The phrase "balancing functional and imperative paradigms" would benefit from further explanation.

- Section 4: Most explanations referring to code snippets do not reference the line numbers in the snippets; aligning the explanations with the line numbers would improve readability.

---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
This paper discusses the translation of Legata regulations (a domain-specific language used for simulations) for preventing collisions in maritime vessels into Rebeca (an actor-based model for formal verification). The translation is performed both manually and with ChatGPT, thus also evaluating the latter approach. The ChatGPT prompt for translation either takes no example (zero-shot) or one example (one-shot).

The contribution of the paper is interesting, as it presents a translation workflow whose source code is made available by the authors. The analysis of using generative AI for the translation is also of value (although the comparison is only made on six clauses). Overall, the paper presents a good amount of work and comparisons.
However, I have found some issues in how these results are presented and motivated.
While Rebeca and Legata are introduced in Section 2, the explanations remain quite abstract and no concrete examples are provided early on. As a result, when the mapping is described later (Section 4.4), it is difficult to follow without already being familiar with the two formalisms. Introducing a small running example earlier in the paper would significantly improve readability.
The translation itself is mainly done manually, with some discussion on examples of translation and some final guidelines scattered across different sections of the paper. The overall structure could be improved by consolidating these parts, as Section 5.2 (manual transformation results) partially overlaps with the methodological discussion in Section 4.
There is an excess of schematization, with many tables, itemised lists, and enumerations, especially towards the end of the paper. Several of these repeat concepts already introduced earlier (e.g., abstraction limits, limitation categories), which affects readability. A more concise and less repetitive presentation would strengthen the paper.

- The second part of the abstract, especially the last sentence, is difficult to understand due to the density of technical terms introduced before a proper context is given.
- Beginning of Section 4: Constructive research methodology. This is explained as creating artifacts (translation rules, models). Given the topic of the article (a translation), the choice of this methodology seems straightforward. Are there other possible alternative approaches?
- Section 4.2, Table 1, and other parts of the paper: it is mentioned that continuous aspects have been discarded since Rebeca is a discrete formalism. Since many aspects of maritime navigation rules involve continuous behaviour, it would be useful to better justify the choice of Rebeca compared to alternative formalisms supporting both formal verification and continuous modelling (e.g., UPPAAL, which is already mentioned in related work).
- Section 4.4 describes the mapping, i.e., the main contribution of the paper. However, at this point there has not yet been a concrete example of a full Legata clause or Rebeca model. Some identifiers (e.g., IstanbulStrait, Map.Sea@TrafficZone) are introduced without sufficient grounding. Table 3 lists the clauses used but does not show them explicitly, referring instead to another paper, which makes the presentation harder to follow.
- Page 7 artifact: I am not aware whether the artifact has been submitted for evaluation, but it is suggested to publish it in a permanent repository rather than only on GitHub.
- Section 5.2 looks partly like a continuation of Section 4, as it further explains the manual translation. The distinction between method and results could be made clearer.
There is extensive use of tables; it is not always clear whether all of them are necessary. For example, Table 6 reports min/max/average scores over multiple runs, but the rationale for repeating each experiment three times and aggregating results could be explained more explicitly in the text.
- Section 5.4: the transformation example should have been anticipated earlier in the paper. Furthermore, Listings 2 and 3 are almost identical; a more complex or diverse example would better highlight the contribution. This is also the first point where actual Legata and Rebeca code is shown, which again comes quite late.
- From Section 6 to the end of the paper, the text becomes more tedious, as it is mostly a sequence of lists and enumerations (also in the conclusions). A more narrative and less repetitive style would improve readability. For example, Section 6.1 contains a summary that largely repeats the preceding paragraphs. Section 6.3 (limitations taxonomy) and Section 6.4 (model extensibility) partially overlap and reiterate similar points (e.g., continuous dynamics, discretisation limits). These sections could be merged or streamlined.
- Section 6.5 discusses transformation guidelines. This part is useful, but it could be better integrated with earlier sections describing the mapping, rather than appearing as a separate, partially repetitive discussion.

---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
Summary:

The paper presents an approach to verifying the behaviour of maritime vessels. The proposed method translates maritime regulations, expressed in the domain-specific language Legata, into assertions that can be model-checked using Rebeca. The authors demonstrate that this translation can be performed both manually and with the assistance of LLMs. While the LLM-supported translation reduces manual effort, it still requires human oversight due to the lack of correctness guarantees.

To illustrate the approach, the authors introduce an abstract model consisting of two vessels moving on a discrete 20×20 grid, on which the generated assertions are verified using Rebeca. The paper also discusses the capabilities and limitations of the approach, with the primary limitation being its restriction to finite-state models.

Evaluation:

The paper addresses an interesting real-world problem, the compliance with maritime regulations, which is approached using formal methods. It can be viewed as a proof of concept demonstrating the feasibility of such an approach, and it has the potential to complement existing simulation-based techniques.

The scope of the translation from regulations to verifiable properties is limited to invariants. The authors provide a convincing discussion of why model checking is not feasible for certain regulations and outline directions for extending the approach to additional classes of properties in future work.

However, the abstract model used for verification is highly simplified. It remains unclear to what extent the verification results obtained for this model generalise to real-world maritime scenarios. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of this issue.

Furthermore, the regulation-to-property translation appears relatively straightforward. The use of LLMs for this task is not convincing, especially given the risk of introducing errors due to hallucinations. It would be more convincing to explore formal, correctness-preserving approaches to automating this translation.

Overall, while the paper approaches an interesting problem and presents a solution for a limited fragment, its practical applicability to real maritime settings remains unclear. In addition, the motivation for using LLMs is not sufficiently justified. Thus, the contribution appears to be a bit too weak for acceptance at COORDINATION.

Minor Comment:

The paper makes extensive use of lists. The presentation would benefit from a more narrative writing style.

---- OVERALL EVALUATION ----
In the paper, two notions of motif refinement are introduced to facilitate a modular and incremental design of controllers in structured cyber-physical systems. Motifs are organised assemblies of subsystems characterised by a hierarchical structure, rendered via interfaces, and responsible for specifying how control commands, outputs and disturbances are propagated through such a hierarchy.
The authors show how to link motifs to linear and hierarchical control, and then propose two notions of refinement of motifs: one defined as a preorder over interfaces, and one as a preorder over maps, i.e., directed graphs with predicates. These notions are then extended to aggregations, control profiles, disturbance profiles, and interaction constraints. i.e., to all the remaining components of a motif.
A notion of simulation over objects (the LTS-like structures associated to motifs) is provided and it is shown that interface refinement preserves simulation. A similar result is shown, under some constraints, for map refinement.
Then, it is shown how control stability is preserved by the various notions of refinement proposed in the paper.


Let us analyse the paper:

- Relevance: The paper fits well the scope of the conference.

- Significance: The paper is of certain interest for the community, and it comes with a timely contribution. The over-increasing complexity of systems (and their controllers) calls for structured formal methods approaches for their analysis and the verification of their correctness.

- Originality: The paper increments on previous work on motifs, but the novelty is substantial and non trivial.

- Soundness: As far as I could check, the results are sound.

- Reproducibility: NA (contribution is theoretical)

- Overall quality: Sometimes the presentation is a bit hard to follow, especially for someone like this reviewer who was not familiar with the concept of motif. However, the paper is generally well written and the examples are insightful.

Based on the considerations above, I recommend acceptance.

Minor comments:

The paper addresses the realisability problem for sets of sequential timed scenarios — a topic in the formal modelling of distributed concurrent systems.


Background and motivation. Building on the authors' earlier work, the paper uses sequential timed scenarios (sequences of events with time constraints) and distributed timed scenarios (DTSs — parallel compositions of sequential scenarios that synchronise on shared events). The key analytical tool is the stable distance relation, which captures ordering dependencies and tight time bounds between events across a set of scenarios.

Contributions.
- The paper defines a new formalism, timed scenario expressions, i.e. expressions, built from DTSs using three operators: choice, concatenation, and parallel composition. The semantics of an expression is the union of the behaviours that are specified by that expression.

 — Realisability algorithm. Given a finite set S of sequential scenarios, the goal is to find an expression E such that the behaviours described by E exactly match those of S.
The central result (Theorem 2) gives necessary and sufficient conditions for S to be realisable by a single DTS.

- Experimental results. The algorithm is compared against an exhaustive breadth-first search for the optimal (minimum-alternatives) solution. The results show the heuristic algorithm is very close to optimal in practice and vastly faster for larger inputs.

Conclusions and future work. The authors plan to extend the framework with the Kleene star operator and to explore machine learning for the realisability problem.

Some questions to address:
- The paper assumes each event occurs exactly once in each scenario (normalised form). How restrictive is this in real-world applications?
- Could the constraint-based and order-based divisions ever conflict or produce circular dependencies in the recursion?
- The comparison with related work (MPST, session types, choreographies) is brief. How does the expressive power compare more formally?
- The experimental results are limited to 5 examples per category. Would a larger benchmark give more confidence in the conclusions?

**Summary**

The paper introduces timed scenario expressions, a formalism for the compositional specification of distributed timed behaviours. It addresses the realisability problem for sets of sequential timed scenarios by identifying necessary and sufficient conditions, including order-closure and constraint compatibility, under which such sets can be realised by a single distributed timed scenario. When these conditions are not satisfied, the authors propose a recursive partitioning algorithm based on ordering and constraint inconsistencies, which constructs a scenario expression that preserves the semantics of the original set.

**Strength**

- The work extends prior foundations by introducing timed scenario expressions, enabling a compositional and structured representation of distributed behaviours.

- The paper is technically sound, with precise definitions and a well-defined semantic framework for reasoning about timed scenarios.

- It provides a clear characterisation of realisability, identifying order-closure and constraint compatibility as necessary and sufficient conditions.

- The approach is constructive, presenting an explicit algorithm that synthesises a scenario expression from a given scenario set.

**Weakness**

- The presentation is dense and notation-heavy, with many interdependent definitions introduced early (even in introduction),
 which makes the paper difficult to follow and obscures the underlying intuition.

- The contribution appears incremental, as the core modelling framework is largely based on prior work, with the main novelty being the introduction of scenario expressions and the associated realisability procedure.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and provides a well-defined formal treatment of the realisability problem, including a clear characterisation and a constructive algorithm. However, the contribution appears incremental, and the presentation is dense, which limits accessibility. More importantly, the strict conditions required for merging scenarios may lead to trivial results, where the constructed expressions offer limited abstraction over the input. Combined with the lack of systematic evaluation, this makes it difficult to assess the practical usefulness of the approach. For these reasons, I consider the paper to be borderline.

SUMMARY

This paper presents a BPMN extension to explicitly model interactions between BPMN choreographies and their environments.  The authors follow a proposal from existing literature, introducing place graphs to define the environment model (locations and connectivity).  They introduce environmental guards (reachability, position and attribute guards) to condition certain tasks and movement messages that instruct participants to change their locations.  Each extension is given a graphical and a textual syntax.  Their semantics is integrated into that of BPMN choreographies in the style of Petri nets.  The extension is illustrated by an emergency response choreography scenario.

MAJOR COMMENTS

The authors claim that they explicitly model the interaction between the choreography and the environment.  They do so by separating the semantics into two layers: endogenous and exogenous.  The endogenous layer models the evolution of the choreography configurations.  The exogenous layer extends the endogenous one by introducing and keeping track of the environment model.  This extension is done by adding a single rule

<C, G, \sigma> --> <C, evolveG(G, \sigma), evolveS(G, \sigma)>

However the two functions, evolveG and evolveS, are left unspecified, with only a minimal default definitions.  Since place graphs were proposed in prior work, this reduces -- in my view -- the overall contribution to its syntactic part, i.e. introducing the environmental guards and mouvement messages.  Although the concluding section mentions a number of avenues for further research, by itself, this contribution does not seem significant enough for a separate publication.

SUMMARY

relevance: well in the scope
significance: has promising avenues for research
originality: relies heavily on prior work
soundness: N/A
reproducibility: N/A
overall quality: promising but not yet sufficient for acceptance

DETAILED COMMENTS

Why are sequence flows called "sequence flows"? To me, they appear to be nodes in the choreography and places in the corresponding Petri net.  I see tokens flowing through those nodes but I do not see themselves flowing in any sense.

Many BPMN represent behaviors that are supposed to occur in an environment that conditions its execution. On the other hand, such environments also condition the occurrence of interactions, modeled as choreographies.
  The paper presents an extension of BPMN choreographies to make them environment aware. The underlying idea is that participants of a choreography  might alter the environment in which the collaboration takes place and vice versa. According to the authors, there have been initiatives in this direction but none manage to capture the bidirectional nature of the influence exerted by the participants over the environment and vice versa.

In favor:
	- The authors identify a problem that, while occurs naturally in real life scenarios, they claim it is not tackled appropriately by any previous work in the field.
	- The proposed solution is technically correct.

Against:
	- It seems to me that the characterization of real life environment require a huge amount of work due to the number of attributes that might be needed to completely characterize real life places and situations.
	- The solution proposed by the authors seems to be borrowed from a subset of the papers that they characterize as being ill fitted for the the task. A deeper critique to the pros and cons of the preexisting proposals might lead to a more natural presentation of the fusion proposed.
	- The use of “|” for choreography sequential composition is quite misleading because it is usually used for a choice instead of sequentialization, generally represented by “;”
	- The semantics is quite complicated, logically, it has to provide an appropriate interpretation to a complex language. The main drawback regarding this is the lack of intuitions behind each device used to build it.
	- The discussion of related work fails to dig in the similarities and differences; for example, the sentence “Similarly, [4] introduces a new type of event, the location event, triggered by environmental conditions captured by IoT sensors.” does not state a clear comparison. I can imagine that, while in [4] a very specific time of event changing the environment is proposed, in the author’s proposal this is dealed with in a natural way as part of a more general mechanism for expressing changes in the environment, which is characterized by locations, an accessibility relation and a collection of attributes taking values of the support of a specific  algebra; for example, the treatment of natural numbers for people in locations. None of this is properly explained. Other comparisons has the same issue.
	- I cannot see a difference between “Finally, the authors of [7,8] introduce environment-aware BPMN collaboration models to capture and leverage the interplay with the environment. This is accomplished by combining traditional BPMN collaborations with an explicit representation of the environment as semantically enriched place graphs.” and the proposal contained in the paper. The answer to this comes only at the end of the section.

	In my opinion, the most important results behind the author’s proposal are the ones left for future work. In some sense, the previous sections of the paper fall a bit short by only providing a language definition with a potential application, but quite detached from any computational activity.
