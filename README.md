### 2023 PREFACE (would follow it as it provided topical headinghs)

This volume contains the proceedings of the 25th International Conference on Coordination Models and Languages (COORDINATION 2023), held during June 19–23, 2023, at NOVA University Lisbon, in Lisbon, Portugal, as part of the 18th International Federated Conference on Distributed Computing Techniques (DisCoTec 2023).

Modern information systems rely increasingly on combining concurrent, distributed, mobile, adaptive, reconfigurable, and heterogeneous components. New models, architectures, languages, and verification techniques are necessary to cope with the complexity induced by the demands of today’s software development. Coordination languages have emerged as a successful approach, in that they provide abstractions that cleanly separate behavior from communication, thereby increasing modularity, simplifying reasoning, and ultimately enhancing software development. COORDINATION provides a wellestablished forum for the community of researchers interested in models, languages, architectures, and implementation techniques for coordination.

COORDINATION 2023 solicited contributions in five different categories: (1) long regular papers describing thorough and complete research results and experience reports; (2) short regular papers describing research in progress or opinion papers on past coordination research, on the current state of the art, or on prospects for the years to come; (3) short tool papers describing technological artefacts in the scope of the research topics of coordination; (4) long tool papers describing technological artefacts in the scope of the research topics of coordination; and (5) survey papers describing important results and success stories that originated in the context of coordination.

There were 27 paper submissions distributed over the different categories: 14 long regular papers, seven long tool papers, four short regular papers, and two short tool papers. The selection of the papers was entrusted to the Program Committee (PC), with 29 members from 15 different countries. The selection of the papers was done electronically, in two phases. In the first phase, which lasted three weeks, each submission was singleblind reviewed by at least three program committee members, in some cases with the help of external reviewers. During the second phase, which lasted slightly less than one week, the papers were throughly discussed. The decision to accept or reject a paper was based not only on the review reports and scores but also on these in-depth discussions.
In the end, 14 papers were selected to be presented at the conference: eight long regular papers, four long tool papers and two short tool papers.
 
The authors of the accepted papers were subsequently invited to participate in the EAPLS artefact badging. The 11 members of the Artefact Evaluation Committee (AEC), chaired by Alceste Scalas, awarded the available badge to eight artefacts, the reusable badge to five artefacts, and the functional badge to three artefacts.

In addition to the selected papers, these proceedings contain a paper that accompanies the excellent invited talk by Frank Pfenning from Carnegie Mellon University, USA, entitled “Relating Message Passing and Shared Memory, Proof-Theoretically”. This paper was subject to a different review process, carried out by the PC chairs.
 
We are grateful to all involved in COORDINATION 2023. In particular, to the authors for their submissions, the attendees of the conference for their participation, the PC members and external reviewers for their work in reviewing submissions and participating in the discussions, the AEC members for their effort in the evaluation of the artefacts, and the Steering Committee, chaired by Mieke Massink, for their guidance and support.
We are also grateful to the Organizing Committee, chaired by Carla Ferreira, for their excellent job. We also thank the providers of EasyChair Conference Management System, which was a great help in organizing the submission and reviewing process and in the preparation of the proceedings. We would also like to acknowledge the prompt and professional support from Springer, who published these proceedings in printed and electronic volumes as part of their LNCS and LNPSE book series.

### 2025 PREFACE 

Preface
This volume contains the papers presented at COORDINATION 2025: the 27th International Conference on Coordination Models and Languages, held on June 17–19, 2025, in Lille.
 
Modern information systems rely increasingly on combining concurrent, distributed, mobile, adaptive, reconfigurable, and heterogeneous components. New models, architectures, languages, and verification techniques are necessary to cope with the complexity induced by the demands of today’s software development. Coordination languages have emerged as a successful approach, in that they provide abstractions that cleanly separate behaviour from communication, thereby increasing modularity, simplifying reasoning, and ultimately enhancing software development. COORDINATION provides a wellestablished forum for the community of researchers interested in models, languages, architectures, and implementation techniques for coordination.
 
COORDINATION 2025 solicited contributions in three different categories: (1) regular papers describing thorough and complete research results and experience reports; (2) tool papers describing technological artefacts in the scope of the research topics of Coordination; and (3) survey papers describing important results and success stories that originated in the context of coordination. Artefacts submitted with papers were also evaluated by three members of the Artefact Evaluation Committee, which awarded EAPLS badges for being Available, Functional, Reusable, and with results Reproducible.
 
There were 27 submissions. Three program committee members single-blindly reviewed each submission. The committee decided to accept 12 papers, 10 regular and 2 tool papers. The program also included 1 invited talk and 1 invited tutorial.
 
Four accepted papers are on choreographic approaches to model and validate concurrent and distributed systems. Christopher Esterhuyse, Benjamin Lion, Hans-Dieter Hiep, and Farhad Arbab authored the paper “Formal Foundations for Reowolf: MultiParty Sessions via Synchronous Protocol Programming”, awarded two badges (available and functional), which defines formally the semantics of Reowolf connectors to compose message-passing multiparty applications defined with the synchronous protocol description language PDL. The correctness criteria of PDL runtime systems and the connector runtime reference implementation were machine-checked. Robert Rubbens, Petra van den Bos, and Marieke Huisman authored the paper “Verified Parameterized Choreographies”, awarded two badges (available and functional), which presents an extension of the choreographic verification platform VeyMont with parameterized endpoint families and parameterized communication. The semantics of the language is defined by a choreographic projection, which encodes a parameterized choreography into a single PVL program that the platform VerCors can verify. This new version of VeyMont also generates provably correct code. Nikolaus Huber, Susanne Graf, Philipp Rümmer, and Wang Yi authored the paper “MIMOSA: A Language for Asynchronous Implementation of Embedded Systems Software”, awarded two badges (available and reusable), which presents the Mimosa language for the design and implementation of asynchronous reactive systems communicating through FIFO buffers. The semantics supports side-effectful computations and has an asynchronous coordination layer to orchestrate processes. Interested readers can experiment with an interpreter. Tilman Zuckmantel, Thomas Hildebrandt, Yongluan Zhou, and Boris Düdder authored the paper “DACEO: Declarative Asynchronous Choreographies with Data-dependent Event Ordering”, which presents a formal modelling language designed for declarative asynchronous choreographies with data-dependent message ordering and data objects which enforce causal consistency constraints in an asynchronous, event-based micro-services architecture.
 
On the orchestrator-based view of modelling distributed systems, there are two papers. Giorgio Delzanno, Cosimo Laneve, Arnaud Sangnier, and Gianluigi Zavattaro authored the paper “Decidability Problems for Micro-Stipula”, which studies the borders between decidability and undecidability of determining whether a clause is never executed in Micro-Stipula, a language to describe smart contracts. Carlos Gustavo Lopez Pombo, Agustín Eloy Martinez-Suñé, Hernan Melgratti, Diego Senarruzza Anabia, and Emilio Tuosto authored the paper “Behavioural, Functional, and Non-Functional Contracts for Dynamic Selection of Services”, which discusses bisimulation techniques for automata models for expressing service contracts and checks that the interoperability can be guaranteed even when contracts use mismatching names.
 
Two Tool papers are included in these proceedings. José Proença and Maurice H.
 ter Beek present “RebeCaos”, awarded two badges (available and functional), which describes a web-based front-end tool for the Rebeca language. Rebeca is an actor-based language for modelling and analysing concurrent and distributed systems. RebeCaos can simulate different operational semantics of Rebeca, together with some reachability analyses. Gianluca Aguzzi, Lorenzo Bacchini, Martina Baiardi, Roberto Casadei, Angela Cortecchia, Davide Domini, Nicolas Farabegoli, Danilo Pianini, and Mirko Viroli present “A Demonstrator Toolchain for Self-organising Robot Teams”, awarded two badges (available and reusable), which describes a demonstrator for deploying aggregate computing solutions in robot coordination scenarios, addressing key requirements such as localisation, program distribution, and hardware abstraction.
 
Furthermore, there is a work by Alessandro Aldini and Claudio Antares Mezzina on “Formalizing Errors in CCS with 3-Valued Logic”, which adds to the process algebra CCS a term to characterise error configurations in concurrent systems, based on McCarthy’s conjunction in three-valued logic. The work laid out includes an operational semantics, a sound axiomatization for bisimulation, an HML characterisation of the bisimulation, and a case study. A paper by Fabio Gadducci, Carlos Olarte, and Frank Valencia proposes “A Constraint Opinion Model” that generalises the standard DeGroot opinion model by representing agents’ opinions and influences as soft constraints rather than single real values. The resulting model is now able to consider agents sharing partial information and preferences, engaging in discussions on multiple topics simultaneously, and representing opinions with different degrees of uncertainty, including imposing them. Moreover, Antonio Brogi, Roberto Casadei, Nicolas Farabegoli, Stefano Forti, and Mirko Viroli describe a “Declarative Deployment Planning for Green Pulverised Collective Computational Systems”, an approach and toolchain based on simulation, coupled with a Prolog-based planner to guide the search for candidate deployments of pulverised systems. The work shows the declarativity, modularity, scalability, and amenability of the setting for continuous reasoning of deployment alternatives. Finally, Carolyn Talcott develops “Dialects for the CoAP IoT Messaging Protocol”, focusing on the Constrained Application messaging protocol, giving a specification of a resourcelimited reactive attack model and its reachability analysis, demonstrating reactive attacks and their mitigation by dialecting.

We also thank the providers of the EasyChair Conference Management System, which was a great help in organising the submission and reviewing process and in the preparation of this proceedings volume.
